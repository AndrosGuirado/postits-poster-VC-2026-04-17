# Writing Guidelines — Extended Reference

## Proposals

### Structure
1. Point first. What we're proposing and why it matters.
2. What we'll do. Clear scope, no fluff.
3. How we'll do it. Process only if it adds confidence.
4. What it costs. Straightforward.

### Example — Good
"We'll redesign the product detail pages for mobile, focusing on reducing the 3-step add-to-cart flow to one tap. Based on the analytics you shared, this addresses the 40% mobile drop-off directly."

### Example — Bad
"We propose a holistic reimagining of the mobile commerce experience, leveraging our deep expertise in user-centered design to create a seamless, best-in-class shopping journey."

## Case Studies

### Structure
1. What the client does (one line)
2. The challenge
3. What we made
4. The result (concrete if possible)

### Tone
Third person for the client, first person plural for us only when necessary. Let the work speak. Use pull quotes from the client if available.

## Email

### Rules
- Get to the point in the first sentence
- One ask per email when possible
- No "I hope this email finds you well"
- Sign off: "R." or first name, nothing elaborate
- If it's longer than a screen, it should probably be a doc

### Example — Good
"Hey [Name],

Quick one — we've wrapped the initial concepts for the homepage. Three directions, each with a different entry point for the collection.

Link to review: [link]

Happy to walk through on a call if useful, but they should be self-explanatory.

R."

### Example — Bad
"Dear [Name],

I hope you're doing well! I wanted to reach out to share some exciting updates regarding the homepage design project. Our talented team has been working tirelessly to develop innovative concepts that we believe will truly transform the digital experience for your customers..."

## Social Media

### LinkedIn
- Position as resource, not promoter
- Share thinking, process, or a specific insight
- No "We're thrilled to announce" or "Excited to share"
- Keep it under 150 words unless the content genuinely warrants more

### Instagram
- Visual-first. Caption supports, doesn't repeat
- Casual, specific, human
- No hashtag walls. 3-5 max, and only if they're genuinely useful

## Slack (Internal)

- Direct and casual
- Emojis are fine, used naturally
- No corporate announcements dressed up as casual messages
- Thread when the topic needs depth
- React instead of reply when a reaction says it all
